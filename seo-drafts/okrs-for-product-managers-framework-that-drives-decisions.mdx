# OKRs for Product Managers: The Framework That Actually Drives Product Decisions

Most product teams write OKRs every quarter. Most of them don't look at them again until the next planning cycle. That's not a motivation problem — it's a writing problem.

Done right, OKRs don't just describe where you want to go. They answer the question you face every day: "Should we build this, or that?" If your OKRs can't answer that question, they're decorative.

Here's how to write OKRs that actually run your prioritization.

## OKRs vs KPIs: The Distinction That Changes Everything

Every PM knows what KPIs are. The confusion comes from mixing the two systems.

**KPIs are health indicators.** They tell you if the business is running normally. Monthly active users, churn rate, support ticket volume — these should be tracked continuously and shouldn't change quarter to quarter without a major strategic shift.

**OKRs are change drivers.** They describe a specific improvement you're committing to over 60–90 days. An OKR should expire. A KPI should not.

The most common OKR mistake is writing KPIs disguised as OKRs: "Maintain MAU above 500K." That's not an objective — it's a monitoring threshold. A real objective sounds like: "Make our onboarding so fast and clear that new users reach their first 'aha moment' before they have time to doubt their signup decision."

**Output vs Outcome: The Line That Separates Good from Bad OKRs**

A bad Key Result: "Launch the new AI-powered search feature."

A good Key Result: "Increase search-to-result-click rate from 38% to 55% among new users."

The first measures your activity. The second measures the impact of your activity on a real user behavior. One of these tells you whether you shipped. The other tells you whether it mattered.

## The Three-Level OKR Hierarchy

OKRs work at three levels simultaneously, and confusion about which level you're at is why most teams feel like OKRs create overhead without clarity.

**Company OKRs** (set by leadership, 3–5 per year): Directional and qualitative. "Become the default tool for async collaboration in engineering teams." You don't own these — you align to them.

**Team OKRs** (owned by the PM, quarterly): The translation layer. Given the company direction, what will your product team specifically accomplish this quarter that contributes meaningfully? This is where you make the bet.

**Individual OKRs** (optional, sometimes useful): For senior ICs or PMs working on initiative-level focus, but don't over-engineer this layer — it creates bureaucracy without clarity at scale.

As a PM, your primary job in OKR season is to set the Team OKR layer. That means: given the company direction, what is the specific, measurable change in user behavior or business outcome that your team can credibly drive in 90 days?

## Writing Key Results That Actually Work

A Key Result should:
- Be a **number with a start and end state** ("from X to Y by date")
- Measure an **outcome**, not an output
- Be **uncomfortable to commit to** — if you're certain you'll hit it, it's not stretching
- Be **legible to a non-PM** — "increase P90 query latency" fails this; "reduce checkout errors from 7% to under 2%" passes

### The Spotify Example

Spotify doesn't just track whether Discover Weekly was shipped — they track Playlist Save Rate (users who save a generated playlist), which is a behavioral proxy for "did this feel valuable?" The KR is the outcome, not the release.

Apply the same logic to your team's work. Don't write "ship the redesigned onboarding flow." Write "increase day-7 retention from 31% to 42% for users who complete onboarding."

### The 70% Rule

OKRs introduced by Google's John Doerr include an important norm: a well-calibrated OKR should be hit about 70% of the time. If you're hitting 100%, you're not stretching. If you're hitting 40%, you're either overreaching or the goal was arbitrary.

The 70% rule matters for product culture: it signals that ambitious commitments are valued over sandbagged ones. When you see a team that always hits 100% of OKRs, they're playing not to lose, not to win.

## Three OKR Anti-Patterns PMs Fall Into

### 1. The Roadmap-as-OKR Trap
"Ship the self-serve onboarding flow, the referral program, and the mobile app by Q3." That's a roadmap. OKRs describe why those things matter — not what you plan to ship.

### 2. Too Many Objectives
Three objectives per team per quarter is the upper limit. Five is chaos. Each objective requires focused prioritization energy. Dilute it across six objectives and you've created a to-do list, not a strategy.

### 3. No Clear Owner
Every Key Result should have one person who will be embarrassed if it's missed. "The team" owns a metric means no one does. Name the owner, even on small teams.

## Running OKR Cycles as a PM: The Weekly Habit

The OKR cycle only works if you check in on Key Results every week — not as a status update, but as a decision trigger.

Each week ask: "Given where this KR is tracking, does it change what we work on this sprint?" If the answer is always no, your OKRs aren't running your prioritization — they're running alongside it, which is just performative planning.

The check-in should take 10 minutes in your weekly sync. Update the current number. Name the one thing that would most move it this week. That's it.

## What to Do Before Your Next Planning Cycle

1. Take your last quarter's OKRs. For each KR, ask: "Did we actually make decisions based on this?" If not, rewrite it so you would.
2. Pick no more than two objectives for next quarter. Write a first draft of each KR as an output, then rewrite it as an outcome.
3. For each KR, name the leading indicator — the early signal that tells you in week 2 whether you're on track, not week 10.

OKRs aren't a planning exercise. They're a decision-making framework that needs to be loaded into every backlog discussion, roadmap review, and sprint retro.
